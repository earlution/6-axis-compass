## Three Open Questions — Decisions

### OQ1 — The axis name

**Decision:** **Ratified** — *libertarian/authoritarian axis*

**Rationale:** The term is canonical in comparative politics literature (Kriesi, Inglehart, etc.) and was used in the original multi-axis models predating this project. The contemporary right-coded reading of "libertarian" in American usage is acknowledged but not dispositive: the term's usage in this framework is explicitly defined in §3.2 to mean "consent as the primary organising value" (not a party affiliation), and that definition is clear from the axis description. The term fits the existing "X axis" lexical pattern. No alternative carries fewer disadvantages.

---

### OQ2 — The axis order

**Decision:** **Ratified** — Fifth slot (cultural, economic, military, sovereignty, libertarian/authoritarian, class)

**Rationale:** Placing the new axis in the fifth slot (replacing the deprecated liberty axis) is the least disruptive option. It preserves the existing pedagogical sequence in Episode 00 Segment 3, requires minimal reordering in downstream references, and maintains the logical grouping: economic and military axes together (describing the post-Bretton Woods settlement's external/structural logic), then authority-structure axes (sovereignty and libertarian/authoritarian), then distributional axes (class). The alternative of placing it last or pairing it with sovereignty would require more extensive downstream restructuring for equivalent analytical benefit.

---

### OQ3 — The disposition of existing liberty axis vocabulary

**Decision:** **Class-axis absorption** — the class axis paragraph absorbs the specifically class-axis content of the deprecated liberty axis; axis-conflation rhetoric is deferred to a future dedicated treatment.

**Rationale:** The supplement §V argues that the vocabulary's home is in a treatment of axis-conflation rhetoric — the rhetorical mechanism by which a single word ("free," "liberty") performs claims on multiple axes simultaneously. That argument is analytically sound. However, the axis-conflation rhetoric treatment (the *free*-prefixed family: *free trade*, *free markets*, *free people*, *free countries*) is substantial enough to warrant its own pedagogical space — either as a dedicated Episode 00 sub-segment (after Segment 3, before Segment 5) or as a full episode later in the series. The decision on placement is deferred to the broader Episode 00 / series structure review.

In the meantime, the specifically class-axis content of the deprecated liberty axis — the observation that concentrated private power (corporate monopoly, financial dominance, debt bondage) functions as a structural constraint on freedom — is properly understood as the class-axis position expressed in the vocabulary of freedom rather than in the vocabulary of interests. This content is folded into the class axis treatment in §3.2 of the Academic Paper, with explicit reference to the liberal/liberal conflation (political liberalism vs. economic liberalism) as a worked example of how the same word can perform claims on different axes.

This approach respects the supplement's analytical conclusion (that the liberty-axis work is actually class-axis work expressed in a different register) while deferring the axis-conflation rhetoric treatment to a moment when the show has dedicated space for it.
