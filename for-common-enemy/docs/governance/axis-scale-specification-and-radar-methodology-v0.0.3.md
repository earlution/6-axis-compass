## Preamble

This document specifies the analytical scaffolding required to use the six-axis framework as a *measurement* instrument rather than only as a discursive heuristic. Three commitments follow from the decision to introduce radar diagrams as a regular analytical artefact in this project.

First, every axis must have a defined scale. Saying that an actor is "strongly culturally nationalist" is not, by itself, a defensible coordinate. The framework must specify what a 1 on the cultural axis means, what a 5 means, and what a 10 means, with worked examples at each anchor so that the coding is reproducible by a second analyst working from the same evidence.

Second, every actor must be mapped twice. The show has been arguing since Episode 1 that the most important political phenomenon in contemporary Britain is the gap between what political actors say and what they structurally do. That argument cannot be made honestly while the analytical instrument records only one position per actor per axis. From this version forward, every actor is plotted on two radars: a *declared* radar derived from stated positions, manifestos, and speeches; and a *structural* radar derived from legislative record, governing behaviour, and material policy effect. The delta between the two is the framework's measurement of the rhetoric/practice gap that the show is built around.

Third, no actor may be plotted on a radar without source-backed coordinates entered into the Reference Framework. The radar is a discipline on the framework's evidential standards, not a relaxation of them. A coordinate without a citation is a guess; this document does not authorise guesses.

This is a `MINOR`-class document under the project's versioning policy. It does not alter the six-axis framework's content; it specifies the operationalisation of that framework into a measurement instrument. It depends on the prior adoption of the libertarian/authoritarian axis restructure proposed in *Defining Principles — Supplement v0.0.1*; if that restructure is not adopted, this document requires revision to substitute the liberty axis at position 5.

## I. The Two-Register Methodology

Every political actor occupies two positions on each axis. The two positions are not always the same. The framework names them as follows.

**Declared position.** What an actor says about an axis. The evidence base is verbal: manifestos, party platforms, speeches, published programmes, official statements, leadership communications. The declared position is what the actor invites observers to record. It is not, in itself, dishonest — declared positions are often sincerely held — but it is also not, by itself, evidence of what the actor will do when given the power to act.

**Structural position.** What an actor does on an axis when in a position to act on it. The evidence base is behavioural: legislative record, voting record, governing decisions, policy outcomes, fiscal allocations, appointments, the disposition of state and institutional resources during periods of office. The structural position is what the actor's record establishes regardless of what the actor invited observers to record.

The two registers are not in competition. They are measuring different things, and the framework is interested in both. The framework's central analytical claim — that the political centre's operating logic is the systematic divergence between cultural-axis declaration and economic-axis structural behaviour — is a *delta* claim between the two registers. It cannot be made without the two registers being measured separately.

### The delta as a measurement

The delta between an actor's declared and structural positions on a given axis is itself a measurement. A small delta indicates an actor whose declared and structural positions are aligned: what they say is what they do, regardless of whether the alignment is endorsed by the observer. A large delta indicates an actor whose declared and structural positions diverge: there is an analytically interesting reason for the divergence, and the framework's job is to identify it.

The framework recognises three diagnostic patterns in large deltas, listed in descending order of frequency in the British political record:

1. **Strategic conflation.** The actor pursues structural goals on one axis under declared cover from another axis. The centre's combination of cultural-nationalist declaration and economic-internationalist structural practice is the canonical case. The actor is not lying about the cultural axis; the actor is using the cultural axis to provide moral cover for an economic axis position that would be less defensible if stated directly.

2. **Ideological capitulation.** The actor's declared position remains stable while the actor's structural position drifts toward an institutional or international consensus. The Labour Party's structural alignment with the post-Bretton Woods settlement from 1994 onward, while retaining declared social-democratic commitments, is the canonical case. The delta opens not because the actor is concealing anything, but because the actor has come to believe that the structural position is the only available one.

3. **Performed opposition.** The actor's declared position is more oppositional than the actor's structural position permits. Restore Britain on the economic axis — culturally protectionist declaration, libertarian-internationalist programme — may be the contemporary British case. The delta opens because the declared opposition is electoral signalling and the structural position is the actor's actual operating commitment.

The three patterns require separate empirical evidence and produce separate analytical implications. The radar measures the delta; the source base in the Reference Framework establishes which of the three patterns the delta belongs to.

### Evidential rules for each register

**Declared position is evidenced from:** party manifestos in the relevant electoral cycle; published programmes and policy papers; leadership speeches at party conference or in parliamentary debate; official statements during the period in question; the actor's own stated definition of their position when given the opportunity to do so. Where an actor's declared position has shifted across the period under analysis, the analysis specifies the period and the declared position at the start and end of it.

**Structural position is evidenced from:** legislative record, voting record, the disposition of state resources during periods of office (fiscal allocations, regulatory decisions, appointments); for parties in opposition, the parliamentary record (private members' bills, amendments tabled, voting record on substantive divisions); the policy effect of decisions made during periods of office, including the distributional record where applicable. Where structural position cannot be evidenced because the actor has not held office, the framework records a *constrained* coordinate based on the strongest available proxies (parliamentary record, statements of intent in policy documents) and flags the limitation explicitly.

The two registers are evidenced in separate columns of the Reference Framework. A coordinate that appears on a radar without a corresponding entry in both columns is invalid.

## II. The Scale

Each axis runs from 0 to 10. The scale is *positional*, not *intensive*: a position of 8 on the cultural axis means the actor is closer to the cultural-nationalist pole than to the cultural-internationalist pole, not that the actor is "more intense" about the cultural axis than an actor at 5. Intensity — the degree of political salience the actor assigns to the axis — is a separate property and is not captured by the radar geometry. It is recorded in the Reference Framework as a separate field where it matters analytically.

By convention, **0 is the pole the show is structurally less critical of** and **10 is the pole the show is structurally critical of**. This is an editorial choice with a rhetorical consequence: actors whose structural position aligns with the post-Bretton Woods settlement will tend to produce polygons that push outward toward the edge of the radar, while actors whose structural position opposes the settlement will tend to produce polygons that cluster toward the centre. The visual reading is therefore: the further from the centre of the radar, the further from the show's structural position. This is not a moral judgement; it is a consistent convention so that the radar is readable at a glance.

The pole assignment for each axis, with the value-0 pole listed first:

| Axis | 0 pole | 10 pole |
|---|---|---|
| Cultural | Internationalism / pluralism | Nationalism / cultural-protectionism |
| Economic | Critique of open capital flows / protected domestic economy | Open capital flows / Washington Consensus alignment |
| Military | Non-interventionism | Active interventionism |
| Sovereignty | National sovereignty | Supranational governance |
| Governance | Libertarian / consent-based / democratic | Authoritarian / hierarchical / illiberal |
| Class | Class-conflict analysis | Class-harmony analysis |

### II(a). Radar spoke distribution

The following axis distribution pattern was ratified at WB-022 / OQ2 (2026-05-17); see `docs/session-notes/wb022-editorial-decisions-session-note.md`. It governs pedagogical and tabular enumeration order, logical grouping of axes, and clockwise spoke order on radar charts. Downstream documents must cite this subsection or the session note — do not paraphrase the order in production prose.

| Slot | Axis | Group |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Cultural | Settlement / post-Bretton Woods framing (four axes introduced first in Defining Principles §IV) |
| 2 | Economic | Settlement external/structural logic — **paired with Military** |
| 3 | Military | Settlement external/structural logic — **paired with Economic** |
| 4 | Sovereignty | Authority-structure — **paired with Governance** |
| 5 | Governance | Authority-structure (formerly libertarian/authoritarian; OQ4 rename) — **paired with Sovereignty** |
| 6 | Class | Distributional |

**Spoke order (clockwise, index 0 first):** Cultural → Economic → Military → Sovereignty → Governance → Class — identical to the list and table order above.

**Default orientation:** `flat` (edge-up hexagon). In the Six-Axis Compass renderer, Chart.js uses a start angle of −60° at index 0, so **Cultural** occupies the top flat edge.

**Scale pole convention:** Unchanged from §II above — **0** is the pole the show is structurally less critical of; **10** is the pole the show is structurally critical of (see Academic Paper Table 1 footnote in §3.3).

A note on the governance axis (pole semantics as formerly Lib/Auth). The 0 pole is *not* the pole the show is uncritical of in absolute terms; the show holds that hard-libertarian formations of both right and left have serious limitations as governing programmes. The pole assignment here is "less critical of in the structural sense relevant to the show's thesis" — that is, the show treats the rejection of liberal democracy as a categorically different and graver problem than its acceptance. The 0 pole therefore includes both democratic-socialist and right-libertarian formations that operate within liberal democracy; the 10 pole captures formations that reject it.

## III. Anchor Specifications

Each axis is anchored at three positions — 0, 5, and 10 — with worked examples for each anchor drawn from the British and historical political record. The anchors are *qualitative descriptors*: an actor is placed at a given anchor when their position matches the anchor description, regardless of the analyst's own view of the position. Intermediate values (2, 3, 7, 8) are placed by relative proximity to the nearest anchor.

The worked examples are drawn from formations whose coordinates are not contested within the literature the project draws from. They are deliberately not drawn from contemporary British parties, because the contemporary parties are the analytical objects this framework is trying to measure. Using them as anchor examples would be circular.

The placement of any specific actor on the scale is performed via the questionnaire specified in *Axis Coding Questionnaire v0.0.1*. The questionnaire breaks each axis into between three and six coding questions, each scored on a 0–4 scale, with the per-axis raw score normalised to this document's 0–10 framework scale. The anchor descriptors below define what each axis is measuring; the questionnaire defines how the measurement is taken. The two documents must be used together — neither is sufficient on its own.

### Cultural axis

**0 — Cultural internationalism / pluralism.** Belonging is primarily a matter of shared humanity. Cultural difference within a polity is treated as a feature, not a problem. Political programme actively opposes discrimination on grounds of origin, ethnicity, culture, religion, or nationality.  
*Worked example:* The 1948 Universal Declaration of Human Rights position; the academic anti-imperialist tradition (Said, Spivak, Hall); the founding documents of the British Race Relations Acts.

**5 — Civic nationalism.** Belonging is defined by shared citizenship and political institutions rather than by ethnicity or culture, but the nation as a category is treated as politically primary. Cultural difference within a polity is tolerated and protected by liberal rights, but cultural homogeneity is not pursued as a political goal.  
*Worked example:* The post-1945 Western European Christian Democratic tradition; the centrist post-war British settlement on national identity from Attlee through to Heath.

**10 — Cultural nationalism / cultural-protectionism.** Belonging is primarily a matter of shared national, ethnic, or cultural identity. The preservation of cultural character is treated as a legitimate and primary political value. Cultural difference within a polity is treated as a problem to be managed, reduced, or reversed.  
*Worked example:* The 1968 Enoch Powell "Rivers of Blood" position; the explicit ethno-cultural nationalist tradition in twentieth-century European political thought.

### Economic axis

**0 — Critique of open capital flows / protected domestic economy.** The political programme treats the free movement of capital across borders as a structural cause of harm, and treats the maintenance of domestic industrial capacity as a legitimate and primary political goal. The Washington Consensus is opposed.  
*Worked example:* The Bretton Woods system as designed in 1944 (capital controls, fixed exchange rates, embedded liberalism); the Attlee government's economic programme 1945–51; the contemporary Mazzucato / Pettifor / Varoufakis tradition.

**5 — Mixed economy with international integration.** The political programme accepts open international markets in goods and services while retaining substantial domestic economic intervention. Capital flows are partially regulated. Industrial policy exists but is constrained by external commitments.  
*Worked example:* The German social market economy in its post-1949 form; the European Union's stated economic model prior to the 1992 Maastricht Treaty.

**10 — Open capital flows / Washington Consensus alignment.** The political programme treats free capital movement, financial deregulation, and the discipline of international markets as primary economic goods. Industrial policy is treated as a distortion. The settlement Williamson named in 1989 is endorsed in substance.  
*Worked example:* The Thatcher government's economic programme from 1979 onward; the IMF structural adjustment programmes of the 1980s and 1990s; the New Labour Treasury settlement from 1997 to 2010.

### Military axis

**0 — Non-interventionism.** Military force is reserved for the genuine defence of the country's territory and people. Overseas interventions are opposed in principle, except where authorised by clear collective security mechanisms and supported by an evidenced case of imminent threat or large-scale humanitarian necessity.  
*Worked example:* The American paleoconservative tradition (Buchanan, *A Republic, Not an Empire*); the Stop the War Coalition position on the Iraq War; the Chilcot Report's findings on the justification for the 2003 invasion.

**5 — Conditional interventionism.** Military force is held to be legitimate in international order when authorised by appropriate multilateral institutions and supported by an evidenced case. Unilateral or coalition-of-the-willing interventions are treated with scepticism but not opposed in principle.  
*Worked example:* The institutional UN Charter position; the early-1990s "responsibility to protect" doctrine prior to its operationalisation in the Iraq and Libya cases.

**10 — Active interventionism.** Military force is held to be a routine instrument of foreign policy, including for purposes of regime change, the maintenance of strategic primacy, and the projection of influence. Coalition-of-the-willing interventions are treated as legitimate when conducted by allied states.  
*Worked example:* The 2002–2003 Bush/Blair position on the Iraq War; the 2011 NATO Libya intervention; the sustained American strategic posture since the end of the Cold War.

### Sovereignty axis

**0 — National sovereignty.** Democratic legitimacy is held to require that decisions be made by accountable national governments. Pooled governance arrangements are treated with scepticism. The nation-state is treated as the primary unit of political authority.  
*Worked example:* The Brexit Vote Leave campaign's central argument; the broader Eurosceptic tradition in twentieth-century British politics; classical realist accounts of international relations.

**5 — Selective pooling.** Pooled governance is accepted in specific functional areas (trade, security, environment) where collective action problems are held to require it, but is resisted where it touches matters held to be properly national. The qualified majority voting versus unanimity distinction matters.  
*Worked example:* The de Gaulle position on European integration; the British "opt-out" pattern under successive governments before Brexit.

**10 — Supranational governance.** Pooled governance is treated as the appropriate response to global problems whose scale exceeds the nation-state. Climate, trade, finance, and security are held to require institutions with binding authority above the nation-state level.  
*Worked example:* The federalist tradition in European political thought; the Habermas position on cosmopolitan constitutional patriotism; the institutionalist tradition in international relations theory.

### Libertarian/Authoritarian axis

**0 — Libertarian / consent-based / democratic.** Political legitimacy is held to derive from the consent of the governed. Hierarchical structures require justification at each level. Liberal democratic constraints — rule of law, minority rights, judicial independence, press freedom — are treated as constitutive of political legitimacy rather than as obstacles to it.  
*Worked example:* The Mill / Rawls liberal-democratic tradition; the anarchist tradition (Kropotkin, Bookchin) on the substantive left of this anchor; the right-libertarian tradition (Nozick, Hayek on his strongest reading) on the right-libertarian side.

**5 — Conditional liberal democracy.** Liberal democratic constraints are accepted in principle but are held to admit of substantial exception in cases of perceived emergency, security threat, or institutional necessity. Press freedom, judicial independence, and minority rights are held in tension with executive authority.  
*Worked example:* The Schmittian "exception" tradition; the institutional pattern of post-war Gaullist France; the post-9/11 American executive-power expansion under both Republican and Democratic administrations.

**10 — Authoritarian / hierarchical / illiberal.** Political legitimacy is held to derive from order, hierarchy, tradition, revolutionary necessity, or the survival of the collective. Liberal democratic constraints are treated as obstacles to be removed or suspended. The active or passive intention to replace liberal democratic arrangements with authoritarian ones is present.  
*Worked example:* The classical fascist tradition (Mussolini, Schmitt's *Concept of the Political*); the Leninist tradition on democratic centralism and the dictatorship of the proletariat; the contemporary illiberal-democratic governance pattern (Orbán's Hungary as canonical case, per Trilling).

### Class axis

**0 — Class-conflict analysis.** The interests of labour and capital are held to be structurally in tension. The wage relation is held to involve an irreducible power imbalance. Political programme treats structural redistribution from capital to labour as a necessary, not optional, feature of just political economy.  
*Worked example:* The Marxist tradition; the post-Keynesian tradition on functional income distribution (Kalecki, Robinson); the contemporary Piketty / Stiglitz / Mazzucato position on capital concentration.

**5 — Conditional class harmony.** Capital and labour are held to have substantially aligned interests under conditions of effective regulation and bargaining institutions. Conflict is held to be possible but is treated as a failure of the institutional framework rather than as a structural feature of the wage relation. The mid-twentieth-century corporatist position.  
*Worked example:* The post-war German co-determination model; the Scandinavian social-democratic settlement in its mid-twentieth-century form; the British post-war settlement from 1945 to 1979 as a less-developed instance.

**10 — Class-harmony analysis.** The interests of capital and labour are held to be fundamentally aligned. The wage relation is held to involve no inherent power imbalance, or such imbalance as exists is held to be optimally addressed by market exit and individual contracting. Political programme treats structural redistribution from capital to labour as illegitimate.  
*Worked example:* The Hayek / Friedman tradition on the market as the appropriate medium of distribution; the foundational documents of the post-1979 Thatcher and Reagan settlements; the Institute of Economic Affairs and similar free-market think-tank tradition.

## IV. The Source-of-Error Catalogue

The scale and the methodology do not dissolve the difficulties of measurement. They make those difficulties explicit and locate them in named places. Six recurrent sources of error require disclosure in any analysis using the radar.

**E1 — Coder bias.** The analyst placing the coordinates is not a neutral instrument. Analyst bias enters at three points: the selection of evidence (which speeches, which votes, which periods), the weighting of evidence (which votes count more than others), and the placement decision at the boundary between anchor descriptors. The protocol response is the inter-coder reliability check specified in §V.

**E2 — Period dependence.** Many actors have moved across the period the analysis covers. The Labour Party in 1979 is not the Labour Party in 2007 is not the Labour Party in 2024. A single coordinate per actor is only defensible if the period of analysis is specified. Where an actor has moved substantially within the period, the analysis records two coordinates, with the dates specified.

**E3 — Internal heterogeneity.** Many actors are coalitions whose internal positions diverge. The Conservative Party from 2016 to 2019 contained both committed Europhile MPs and committed Eurosceptic MPs. A single coordinate for the party as a whole loses this. Where internal heterogeneity is analytically significant, the analysis names the wing whose position is being recorded and the basis for treating that wing as the actor's representative position.

**E4 — Stated-versus-revealed at the structural register.** The structural register is itself susceptible to manipulation. A government can legislate one thing and de-fund or de-prioritise it administratively; a manifesto commitment can be operationalised in a form that bears little resemblance to the headline. The structural register requires the analyst to look at outcomes, not at instruments alone. This is harder than it sounds and is the most frequent source of analytical underdetermination.

**E5 — The Restore Britain problem.** Some actors have not held office in a form that allows the structural register to be evidenced from a governing record. For these actors, the structural position is inferred from the strongest available proxies: parliamentary record where applicable, the personal records of named principals, the policy documents and stated intentions. The framework records a *constrained* coordinate in these cases and flags the constraint explicitly. The Restore Britain case under Lowe is the canonical example, evidenced from Lowe's parliamentary record (the QE ban Bill), the policy positions of named principals, and the City career.

**E6 — The contested-evidence problem.** For some axis positions of some actors, the evidence base is itself contested in the literature. The fascism-as-class-harmony coding is uncontested in the academic literature (Griffin, Paxton, Eatwell), but the popular discourse contests it. The framework records the contested status of the evidence and adopts the academic consensus while flagging the contestation. Where no academic consensus exists, the framework records *both* available codings and flags the disagreement.

## V. The Inter-Coder Reliability Protocol

Where a coordinate is contested or where the source-of-error catalogue identifies a high risk of analyst bias, the framework adopts a two-coder protocol. The protocol works as follows.

1. Coder A places coordinates for the actor across all six axes in both registers, with citations entered into the Reference Framework.
2. Coder B, working independently from the same evidence base, places coordinates for the same actor across all six axes in both registers, with citations entered into the Reference Framework.
3. The two sets of coordinates are compared. Differences of 1 point or less on a 0–10 scale are treated as inter-coder consistent. Differences of 2 points or more trigger a recoding session in which both coders state the basis for their placement and either reach agreement on a single coordinate or record both coordinates with the disagreement flagged.
4. The flagged disagreement becomes part of the radar output: contested coordinates are drawn as a range (e.g. 4–6) rather than as a single point, with the range visible on the radar polygon.

The protocol is not always practicable. Where it is not, the framework applies a one-coder protocol with explicit disclosure: the coordinate is placed by the named analyst, the evidence base is specified, and the framework treats the coordinate as provisional pending external review. The published radar visualisations indicate which coordinates have been inter-coder verified and which have not.

For the contemporary British political analysis that is the project's primary subject, the framework should aim for inter-coder verification of all coordinates for the centre, the right (as defined), the left (as defined), and the extreme. Coordinates for historical anchor examples are treated as established once the anchor descriptors have been agreed.

## VI. What the Radar Cannot Do

The radar is a measurement instrument. It is not, on its own, an analytical conclusion. Three limitations require disclosure whenever the radar is published.

**The radar measures position, not significance.** An actor's polygon shows where the actor sits; it does not show how much that positioning matters to the actor, to the electorate, or to the political outcome. A party whose entire platform is built around a single axis position will produce a polygon that looks similar to a party for whom that axis is a minor concern. The framework records salience separately in the Reference Framework where it matters; the radar geometry alone is insufficient.

**The radar measures coordinates, not causation.** Two actors who occupy similar positions on the radar may have arrived at those positions through entirely different causal routes and may pursue them in entirely different ways. The convergence thesis is not the claim that the right and the left share politics because they share radar positions on two axes; it is the claim that they have arrived at structurally similar diagnostic positions through incompatible analytical traditions, and that this shared diagnosis has political implications that the conventional left-right axis conceals. The radar makes the shared diagnostic visible; it does not, by itself, establish the analytical implications.

**The radar is a snapshot, not a trajectory.** Political actors move. A radar plotted for the Labour Party in 1979 will look different from one plotted in 1997 or in 2024. The framework should publish radars at specified points in time, with the trajectory between them recorded where it matters analytically. The radar is not a portrait of a permanent political identity; it is a measurement at a specified moment.

## VII. Implementation

The following implementation sequence is proposed.

**Phase 1 — Scale ratification.** The scale and anchor specifications in this document are reviewed and ratified, with revisions where the anchor examples are contested or where the worked examples need strengthening. Output: this document at version 0.1.0.

**Phase 2 — Reference Framework expansion.** The Reference Framework is expanded with a coordinate column for each actor across all six axes in both registers, with citations supporting each coordinate. The expansion proceeds actor by actor, starting with the centre, then the nationalist right, then the anti-imperialist left, then the extreme as defined in the show's taxonomy. Output: Reference Framework v0.1.0.

**Phase 3 — Inter-coder verification.** The protocol in §V is applied to the contested coordinates identified during Phase 2. Output: inter-coder report and revised Reference Framework v0.1.1.

**Phase 4 — HTML radar tool.** The existing Six-Axis Compass HTML is rewritten to read coordinates from the Reference Framework and render the dual-radar visualisation (declared and structural overlaid, with the delta visible). The tool supports comparison views (multiple actors on a single radar) and time-series views (a single actor across multiple snapshots). Output: Six-Axis Compass v0.1.0.

**Phase 5 — Integration into scripts.** The radar diagrams are integrated into the relevant episode scripts as visual aids. The most natural integration points are Episode 00 Segment 3 (the framework itself), Episode 02 (the centre's operating logic), and the episodes covering specific actors (Lowe/Restore Britain in particular).

Each phase produces a versioned output that can be reviewed and either approved or returned for revision. The scaffolding is built upward; nothing depends on a phase that has not been ratified.

## VIII. The Radar as Diagnostic

The radar has a function the framework's prose treatment does not. By forcing the framework to commit to coordinates, the radar exposes the framework's own gaps.

Three gaps were surfaced by the prototype radar drawn during the conversation that led to this document, and they are the early evidence that the diagnostic works.

The first was that the framework had no defended scale. The placement of any actor at any coordinate was authorial assertion, not measurement. This document is the response to that gap.

The second was that the framework treated declared and structural positions as a single coordinate, which is incompatible with the show's central claim about the gap between the two. This document formalises the two-register methodology and makes the delta the framework's primary measurement of the rhetoric/practice problem.

The third was that some axes — the sovereignty axis in particular — may have internal structure that the single-axis treatment cannot represent. The anti-imperialist left's "ambivalence" on sovereignty is not a single coordinate at the midpoint; it is two genuinely different positions held simultaneously, each on a different evidential basis. This document does not resolve that problem but records it as Open Question 1 in §IX.

The radar will surface more gaps as it is applied. Each surfaced gap is an instruction to the framework, not an embarrassment to it. The instrument is doing its job when it tells the framework where it is underdeveloped.

The canonical worked example of the radar diagnostic in action is *Brexit Under the Six-Axis Framework v0.0.1*. That paper applies the methodology specified here, with codings derived from the questionnaire, to a multi-actor coalition (the five components of the Leave coalition and the three components of the Remain coalition). It is the first sustained application of the framework's measurement instrument to a contemporary British political event, and the case the framework had been claiming to explain longest. Subsequent radar applications should be tested against it for methodological consistency.

## IX. Open Questions

Three questions remain open and require editorial decision before the radar methodology is finalised.

**OQ1 — The sovereignty axis decomposition.** The anti-imperialist left's position on sovereignty appears to require two coordinates, not one: scepticism of supranational governance as a vehicle for capital (pulling toward the national pole) and scepticism of the nation-state as historically exclusionary (pulling toward the supranational pole). A single midpoint coordinate loses this information. Options: (a) accept the loss and flag the case as a special instance of E3 (internal heterogeneity), (b) decompose the sovereignty axis into two sub-axes (institutional sovereignty and identitarian sovereignty), (c) introduce a new axis to capture the second dimension. Option (b) is the most analytically precise but expands the framework beyond six axes. Recommendation: adopt (a) for the radar's first publication, with (b) reserved for a future framework revision if the case proves to be more general than the anti-imperialist left.

**OQ2 — The intensity dimension.** The radar measures position but not salience. Some actors are defined by a single axis; some operate across the framework evenly. The radar geometry does not distinguish these cases. Options: (a) accept the loss as part of the radar's stated limitations, (b) encode salience as the *boldness* of the polygon's edge on a given axis, (c) publish a separate salience profile alongside the radar. Recommendation: adopt (a) for the first publication, with (b) tested in the HTML tool.

**OQ3 — The treatment of internally split actors.** The Conservative Party from 2016 to 2019 is the obvious case: a single coordinate misrepresents the actor; two coordinates require a principle for naming the wings; many coordinates make the radar unreadable. Options: (a) record the actor at the position of its electorally dominant wing during the period, with the dissenting wing recorded separately as a footnote, (b) record both wings as named sub-actors, (c) record the formal party position alongside the dominant wing. Recommendation: adopt (a) as default with (b) for cases where the split is the analytical object.

These are decisions about the framework's analytical instrument, not about its substantive thesis. They should be settled in a single editorial review before the Reference Framework expansion in Phase 2 begins.
